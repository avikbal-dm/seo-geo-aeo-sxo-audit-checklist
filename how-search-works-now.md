# How Search Works Now

A plain-language guide to how pages get found, ranked, and cited, across classic search and the new AI answer engines. Read this to understand *why* each checklist item exists.

---

## The shift in one paragraph

For twenty years, search meant one thing: a list of ten blue links, ranked by relevance and authority. You optimized a page, it ranked, people clicked. That system still exists, but it is shrinking. A growing share of searches now end inside an answer, generated by an AI engine that read dozens of pages and wrote a summary, citing a few sources. Getting *ranked* and getting *cited* are now two different games. This guide covers both.

---

## Part 1 — How classic search ranking works

Classic search runs in three stages: crawling, indexing, and ranking.

### Crawling

Search engines send automated bots (Googlebot is the best known) to follow links across the web and fetch pages. If a bot cannot reach a page, that page cannot rank. Crawlability is the foundation everything else sits on.

Things that block or waste crawling:
- A `robots.txt` file that disallows important pages
- Broken internal links and orphan pages with no links pointing to them
- Slow servers that time out before the page loads
- Infinite URL variations (faceted navigation, session parameters) that exhaust the crawl budget

This is why the Technical SEO tab checks crawlability first. Nothing else matters if the bot never arrives.

### Indexing

After crawling, the engine decides whether to store the page in its index, the giant database it searches when someone types a query. A crawled page is not automatically indexed. Pages get left out when they are thin, duplicate, blocked by a `noindex` tag, or judged low value.

Key indexing signals:
- A clear, unique purpose for the page
- A canonical tag that tells the engine which version is the original
- Enough substantive content to be worth storing
- No accidental `noindex` directives

The Technical SEO and On-Page tabs both check indexing, because a page that is crawled but not indexed is invisible.

### Ranking

Once indexed, pages compete for position on each query. Modern ranking is driven by hundreds of signals, but they cluster into a few groups:

**Relevance.** Does the page match what the searcher wants? This is about more than keywords. Engines use language models to understand topics, synonyms, and intent. A page about "running shoes for flat feet" can rank for "best trainers for fallen arches" because the engine understands they mean the same thing.

**Content quality and E-E-A-T.** Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness, and Trustworthiness. Engines try to assess whether content was created by someone who actually knows the subject, demonstrated through depth, accuracy, original insight, author credentials, and corroborating signals across the web. The Content Audit tab is built around this.

**Authority and links.** Backlinks from other reputable sites act as votes of confidence. The quality and relevance of linking sites matters far more than the raw number. Toxic links from spammy sites can hurt. This is what the Backlinks & Authority tab audits.

**User experience signals.** Page speed (Core Web Vitals), mobile-friendliness, and a layout that does not frustrate the visitor. Engines increasingly reward pages people have a good experience on. This is the technical half of what the SXO tab covers.

**Search intent match.** Possibly the most underrated signal. If a query wants a quick definition and your page is a 4,000-word sales pitch, you will lose to a page that gives the definition fast, even if yours is more "optimized." The engine learns intent from what searchers click and stay on. Matching it is the heart of Search Experience Optimization.

## Part 2 — How AI search engines work

AI engines (Google AI Overviews, ChatGPT, Perplexity, Gemini, Copilot) do something fundamentally different. They do not just rank pages. They read them, synthesize an answer, and cite a few sources.

### Retrieval, then generation

Most AI answer engines run a two-step process often called RAG (retrieval-augmented generation):

1. **Retrieval.** The engine searches its index (or the live web) and pulls a set of candidate passages relevant to the question.
2. **Generation.** A language model reads those passages and writes a synthesized answer, citing the sources it leaned on.

This changes the goal. You are no longer trying to be the single best-ranked page. You are trying to be one of the passages the engine retrieves *and* one it chooses to cite when it writes the answer.

### What makes a page retrievable

For the retrieval step, the engine needs to be able to read and understand your content cleanly:

- **Clean, accessible HTML.** Content buried in JavaScript that does not render, or locked behind interactions, may never be retrieved. Many AI crawlers are less capable than Googlebot at rendering.
- **Clear structure.** Headings, short paragraphs, and logical sections help the engine isolate the passage that answers a given question.
- **Crawler access.** AI engines use their own bots (GPTBot, ClaudeBot, PerplexityBot, Google-Extended). If your `robots.txt` blocks them, you opt out of being cited. An `llms.txt` file can help guide them.
- **Self-contained passages.** A passage that makes sense on its own, without needing the whole page for context, is easier to lift into an answer.

This is what the GEO tab checks.

### What makes a page citable

Retrieval gets you into the candidate pool. Citation is about being chosen. The signals that earn citations overlap with classic quality but lean specific ways:

- **Direct answers near the top.** Lead with the answer, then elaborate. AI engines favor passages that state a clear answer up front.
- **Factual, specific, verifiable claims.** Concrete numbers, named sources, and precise statements are easier for an engine to trust and quote than vague generalities.
- **Structured formats.** Lists, tables, step-by-step instructions, and clear definitions are disproportionately likely to be cited because they map cleanly to how an AI composes an answer.
- **Corroboration.** Engines favor claims that agree with other reputable sources. Being the outlier with an unsupported claim rarely earns a citation.
- **Freshness where it matters.** For topics that change, recency raises citation odds.

This is what the AEO and AIO tabs check.

### Google AI Overviews specifically

AI Overviews are Google's AI-generated answers that appear above the classic results. They draw heavily on pages that already rank well, but ranking is not sufficient. To be cited in an AI Overview a page generally needs:

- To rank on the first page for the query or a closely related one
- A passage that directly and concisely answers the question
- Clear structure (a definition, a list, a table) the AI can extract
- Schema markup that helps Google understand the content type
- Strong topical authority on the subject

The AIO tab is the focused checklist for this. The key insight: AI Overviews often pull from pages ranked 1 to 10, so classic ranking and AI citation reinforce each other. You usually cannot win AI Overview citations without also doing the classic SEO work.

## Part 3 — Why both games matter

It is tempting to treat AI search as a replacement for SEO. It is not, at least not yet. Here is how they relate:

- Classic SEO gets you indexed, ranked, and into the candidate pool that AI engines retrieve from. **AI citation usually depends on classic ranking as a prerequisite.**
- AI optimization (GEO, AEO, AIO) determines whether, once retrieved, you get read and cited rather than skipped.
- Search Experience Optimization (SXO) sits across both: matching intent and delivering a good experience helps you rank *and* helps you earn the engagement signals that feed both systems.

The practical takeaway, and the reason this workbook is structured the way it is: do the technical and on-page foundations first, because they feed everything. Then layer the AI-era checks (GEO, AEO, AIO) on top for any page where AI visibility matters. Skipping the foundations to chase AI citations rarely works, because the engines retrieve from the same indexed, well-structured pages that classic search rewards.
